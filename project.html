How could energy help Africa develop?

Aims

The developed world has grown astronomically since the 19th century. Much of this growth came off the back of a coal-fuelled industrial revolution, which lifted millions from poverty and has produced the comfortable lives many of us have today. However, for much of the world, this standard of living is still a far-cry away. Sub-Saharan Africa is the most emphatic case of this. My project aims to assess the extent to which the relationship between energy usage and development still exists and how Sub-Saharan Africa can and should leverage this.

The strength of the relationship

The map below highlights how development varies so drastically around the world and how underdeveloped countries are often geographically group. The almost unanimously light green western Europe and North American regions and the redness of Sub-Saharan Africa epitomise this. I obtained my GeoJSON required for the map from this site and downloaded the HDI data from Our World in Data , which was in long form and did not require cleaning.

The line graphs build on this idea of geographically concentrated development further. They show Sub-Saharan Africa consistently faces the worst outcomes across a variety of metrics. I scraped the data from the World Bank (here) using the World Bank API, specifying to extract only regional level data.

I then investigated whether similar patterns could be seen in energy access. The following map demonstrates how low access to electricity seems to be a problem faced almost uniquely by Sub-Saharan Africa and not other developing regions. I used the same GeoJSON and a CSV downloaded from the World Bank. An issue I came across with both maps was that the country names in my CSV files did not match the country names seen in my GeoJSON. I used this workbook to identify these countries before manually editing my CSV file so the names matched, and the countries would appear on my maps.

After establishing this trend in energy access, I then aimed to test the relationship between development and energy consumption with a regression. I merged CSV files from the World Bank and Our World in Data. Notably, when merging, some countries that did not share a common name in both datasets were lost. I also dropped all rows with variables that showed “NaN” as this could prevent or discredit my results. Given the sample left was still large, this did not significantly disrupt my regression analysis. I then merged this dataframe with a region list so countries from different regions could be distinguished in my visualisation and removed high income countries and one clear anomaly, Turkmenistan, to increase the relevance of the analysis to Sub-Saharan Africa. The workbook used is here . The relationship proved very strong (R2=0.77).

How could Africa source the energy needed for growth?

I then turned to address the question of how Africa could increase their energy consumption. The area chart below demonstrates how Africa has and still does largely rely on non-renewables. However, by adding a drop-down selector, this allowed me to highlight the increasing use of renewables such as solar and wind. I got the data from Our World in Data via CSV download. The data was in wide form so I converted it to long form in this workbook so it could be processed in Vega-Lite. I also renamed the variables with numbers in front to indicate the order in which I wanted them to be stacked in my area chart.

I further investigated the relative growth of different energy sources with an index chart, using the same dataset. This clearly showed how wind and solar have been by far the fastest growing energy sources in Africa recently.

To examine the extent to which renewable energy could play a role in Africa’s development story, I looked at price trends. The line chart below shows how the levelized cost of renewables has consistently fallen, indicating it has become a more viable alternative to non-renewables. The data, from IRENA, was in wide form, so I converted it to long form in this workbook.

My final chart raises the question of whether Sub-Saharan Africa is justified in increasing energy consumption by any means necessary. It displays how Sub-Saharan Africa’s emissions are miniscule compared to a developed region, North America. Therefore, if Africa were to increase their non-renewable energy consumption, the environmental impact would arguably be negligible. I obtained the data by converting data (World Bank API) from XML to JSON format in Vega-Lite directly.

Conclusion

My analysis demonstrates the extremely strong relationship between development and energy. The lack of access to energy could be a key reason why Sub-Saharan African development has lagged behind for so long. I found that only a small proportion of Africa’s current energy is sourced from non-renewables. However, my analysis also suggests a path to a greener future is potentially viable with the growth in renewable energy sources increasing significantly in recent years. Moreover, if development must be based on non-renewables, this would increase CO2 emissions by a relatively small amount. Therefore, is it right for the developed world to intervene and prevent this?
